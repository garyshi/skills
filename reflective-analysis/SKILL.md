# Reflective Analysis

## Objective

Produce a better provisional judgment, not the longest possible report.

This skill helps identify:

* whether the question is framed correctly;
* which assumptions come from inherited consensus;
* which facts or conditions have recently changed;
* how fast the relevant system is changing;
* which trends can reasonably be extrapolated;
* which causal model best explains the observations;
* what remains genuinely uncertain;
* what future evidence would distinguish the alternatives.

Do not optimize for consensus, contrarianism, comprehensiveness, or rhetorical novelty.

Optimize for explanatory power, current relevance, trajectory awareness, and updateability.

## Core discipline

For every important claim, distinguish:

* **Observation**: what appears to have happened;
* **Fact**: what is supported by reliable evidence;
* **Trajectory**: how the relevant variable has changed over time;
* **Extrapolation**: what may follow if the trajectory continues, slows, or accelerates;
* **Model**: the proposed causal explanation;
* **Assumption**: what the explanation depends on;
* **Forecast**: a testable future claim;
* **Judgment**: the current synthesis and confidence.

Do not silently turn one category into another.

Especially avoid treating extrapolation as fact.

## Step 1: Identify the real decision or question

Restate the question in operational terms.

Determine whether the user is primarily asking about:

* what is true;
* why something happened;
* what is likely to happen;
* what action to take;
* how value, risk, or power will be distributed;
* what is already priced or understood;
* what assumptions may be stale.

Separate adjacent questions that require different evidence.

Examples:

* technical feasibility versus adoption speed;
* economic value versus investment return;
* average performance versus tail risk;
* short-term behavior versus structural change;
* individual cases versus system-wide effects;
* current capability versus trajectory;
* value creation versus value capture.

Define ambiguous terms whose meanings could predetermine the answer.

Examples:

* “works”
* “safe enough”
* “reliable”
* “fully automated”
* “productivity”
* “priced in”
* “expert judgment”
* “AI-native”
* “adoption”
* “disruption”

Replace vague terms with observable criteria when possible.

## Step 2: Surface the initial prior

Before extensive research, record a compact internal prior:

* What is the most likely immediate answer?
* Which common narrative supports it?
* When was that narrative formed?
* Which assumptions must remain true?
* Which assumptions may have changed?
* Which part of the answer feels like inherited consensus rather than fresh reasoning?

Treat this prior as a hypothesis, not an anchor to defend.

Do not discard the consensus. State its strongest version.

## Step 3: Check freshness before using evidence

For any fast-moving subject, classify evidence by both date and relevance.

Ask:

* What system, technology, institution, or market regime was studied?
* Has the object of study materially changed since then?
* Is this evidence about current capability, historical capability, adoption, economics, or risk?
* Does the conclusion still apply, or only the underlying mechanism?
* Are newer observations available that directly test the same claim?
* Does this evidence describe a previous generation, a current generation, or a deployment pattern that still persists?

Old evidence is not automatically invalid. But it must not be treated as current evidence merely because it is rigorous.

When recent first-hand observations conflict with older formal evidence:

* preserve the formal evidence as a prior;
* treat the new observation as a possible update;
* investigate whether the underlying system has changed;
* avoid automatically privileging either source.

## Step 4: Analyze trajectory, not just freshness

For very fast-moving subjects, freshness is necessary but insufficient.

Do not ask only:

> What is currently true?

Also ask:

> How fast is it changing, and what would follow if the current rate of change continues, slows, or accelerates?

Classify claims into three layers.

### Layer 1: Current facts

What is directly supported by present evidence?

Examples:

* current product capabilities;
* current deployment data;
* current costs;
* current benchmark results;
* current user behavior;
* current reliability;
* current failure modes;
* current regulatory constraints;
* current business model;
* current capital requirements.

State these as facts only when the evidence supports them.

### Layer 2: Observed trajectory

What has changed over time?

Look for:

* rate of improvement;
* cadence of releases;
* cost decline;
* latency decline;
* reliability improvement;
* adoption growth;
* workflow change;
* user behavior change;
* ecosystem growth;
* capital investment;
* bottlenecks shifting from one layer to another.

Prefer comparing multiple points in time rather than one recent snapshot.

Ask:

* What improved between version N and version N+1?
* Did the improvement come from capability, tooling, data, workflow, infrastructure, regulation, or user adaptation?
* Is the change linear, exponential, stepwise, cyclical, or slowing?
* Are failures being eliminated, displaced, or hidden?
* Which bottlenecks are falling fastest?
* Which bottlenecks are not improving?
* Is the observed trend broad, or concentrated in curated cases?

### Layer 3: Reasonable extrapolation

Make explicit projections from the observed trajectory.

Separate:

* conservative extrapolation;
* base-case extrapolation;
* aggressive extrapolation;
* discontinuity or regime-change scenario.

For each extrapolation, state:

* what trend is being extended;
* over what time horizon;
* what assumptions must remain true;
* what would cause the trend to slow;
* what would cause it to accelerate;
* what evidence would invalidate the extrapolation.

Do not present extrapolation as fact.

Use phrases such as:

* “current evidence supports...”;
* “the observed trajectory suggests...”;
* “a reasonable extrapolation is...”;
* “this requires assuming...”;
* “this would fail if...”.

## Step 5: Evaluate trend quality

Before relying on a trend, evaluate its quality.

### 1. Measurement consistency

Are the data points measuring the same thing over time?

Beware of comparing:

* different benchmarks;
* different task distributions;
* curated demos versus real use;
* lab results versus production behavior;
* gross capability versus net workflow productivity;
* claimed savings versus realized financial outcomes.

### 2. Saturation risk

Is the trend approaching a hard or soft limit?

Possible limits include:

* physics;
* cost;
* data quality;
* latency;
* regulation;
* human trust;
* distribution;
* organizational adoption;
* liability;
* energy and infrastructure;
* adversarial pressure;
* diminishing marginal utility.

### 3. Bottleneck migration

When one bottleneck improves, another may become dominant.

Examples:

* capability improves, but evaluation becomes the bottleneck;
* generation cost falls, but review cost rises;
* automation improves, but integration and permissions become limiting;
* adoption grows, but organizational accountability becomes limiting;
* demand grows, but physical infrastructure becomes limiting;
* a tool becomes easier to use, but distribution becomes the scarce resource.

A good extrapolation tracks where the bottleneck moves.

### 4. Feedback environment

Trends are more likely to continue when feedback is:

* fast;
* frequent;
* low-noise;
* attributable;
* economically rewarded;
* adversarially tested.

Trends are less reliable when errors are silent, delayed, subjective, or hard to attribute.

### 5. Incentive intensity

Ask who has strong incentives to push the trend forward.

Consider:

* capital investment;
* competitive pressure;
* open-source ecosystem;
* user willingness to adopt;
* developer tooling;
* regulatory permission;
* customer pull;
* strategic necessity;
* existential pressure on incumbents.

A trend with strong economic pressure and rapid feedback deserves more extrapolation weight than a trend based mainly on impressive demonstrations.

### 6. Historical analogy

Use analogies carefully.

For each analogy, specify whether it matches:

* the technical learning curve;
* the cost curve;
* the adoption curve;
* the regulatory environment;
* the market structure;
* the feedback loop;
* the value-capture mechanism.

Do not use historical analogy as evidence unless the mechanism matches.

## Step 6: Extrapolate with discipline

When extrapolating, produce at least two versions.

### Mechanical extrapolation

What happens if the measured trend continues?

This is useful for identifying possibility space.

Examples:

* compute cost falls at the recent rate;
* model capability improves at the recent release cadence;
* adoption doubles on the current schedule;
* capital spending continues at the recent growth rate.

Mechanical extrapolation can be useful, but it is not a forecast by itself.

### Constraint-aware extrapolation

What happens after accounting for:

* bottlenecks;
* incentives;
* second-order effects;
* adoption friction;
* regulation;
* competition;
* capital intensity;
* supply constraints;
* customer behavior;
* feedback quality;
* regime changes.

This is usually more decision-relevant.

Compare the two.

If they diverge sharply, the key question is not the trend itself but the constraint that causes divergence.

## Step 7: Build competing causal models

Construct two to four genuinely different explanations.

They must differ in mechanism, not merely in degree.

For each model specify:

* central mechanism;
* assumptions;
* evidence it explains well;
* evidence it fails to explain;
* expected leading indicators;
* conditions under which it would fail.

Do not create additional models once new ones stop changing the decision.

Example model types:

* **Constraint model**: progress is real but bounded by a bottleneck.
* **Capability-transfer model**: progress in one domain reflects more general underlying capability.
* **Workflow-reconstruction model**: the main change comes from redesigning the process, not substituting into the old process.
* **Commoditization model**: value is created but competition passes most of it to customers.
* **Bottleneck-shift model**: the old scarce factor becomes abundant, but a new scarce factor captures value.
* **Regime-change model**: historical evidence is less predictive because the underlying system has changed.
* **Bubble or over-extrapolation model**: observed progress is real but already overcapitalized or overinterpreted.

Choose models appropriate to the question. Do not force every issue into these examples.

## Step 8: Analyze the feedback environment

Determine how errors and successes are discovered.

Consider:

### Feedback speed

How long until the outcome becomes observable?

### Feedback clarity

Can success or failure be attributed to the decision?

### Selection pressure

Do weak approaches get quickly rejected, exploited, or replaced?

### Reversibility

Can mistakes be detected and corrected before major damage?

### Correlation

Could one mistaken method produce the same error across many cases?

### Silent error risk

Can an approach appear successful while producing hidden or delayed damage?

A system can improve rapidly without formal verification when feedback is fast, competitive, and attributable.

A system may remain unreliable even with expert review when errors are delayed, ambiguous, or systematically shared.

## Step 9: Research only discriminating evidence

Do not search broadly for everything relevant to the topic.

Search for evidence that could distinguish the competing models.

Prioritize:

1. direct observations and primary sources;
2. recent changes in behavior or outcomes;
3. evidence from environments resembling the real use case;
4. failure cases and hidden costs;
5. data that contradicts the leading model;
6. evidence about bottlenecks, not just progress;
7. evidence about value capture, not just value creation.

For each research branch ask:

> What conclusion would change depending on what I find?

If the answer is “none,” stop that branch.

## Step 10: Use agents selectively

Do not create agents merely to increase perspective count.

Use an agent only when it can perform a substantially independent task, such as:

* collecting recent primary evidence;
* evaluating evidence freshness and system comparability;
* analyzing trajectory and rate of change;
* constructing an alternative causal model;
* analyzing incentives or market structure;
* reconstructing a real workflow;
* attempting to falsify a specific thesis;
* evaluating whether a trend is approaching a constraint;
* analyzing value creation versus value capture.

Agents should work independently before seeing one another's conclusions when correlated framing is a major risk.

Do not use majority vote.

Do not ask every agent to research the full question.

Default deep-mode agent set:

1. **Fresh Evidence Agent**
   Finds current facts, recent changes, and primary evidence.

2. **Trajectory Agent**
   Assesses rate of change, release cadence, cost/capability curves, bottleneck migration, and extrapolation quality.

3. **First-Principles Model Agent**
   Builds causal models from constraints, incentives, and mechanisms, without leaning on consensus commentary.

4. **Adversarial Falsifier**
   Searches for counterevidence, hidden costs, stale analogies, correlated failures, and alternative explanations.

Optional agents:

* **Workflow Analyst** for process-heavy domains;
* **Market / Value-Capture Analyst** for investment or strategy questions;
* **Regulatory / Institutional Analyst** for high-liability or policy domains;
* **Customer / User Behavior Analyst** for product adoption questions.

## Step 11: Run a framing review

Before synthesis, ask:

* Am I answering the original question or a nearby easier one?
* Did the initial framing determine the conclusion?
* Am I comparing reality with an idealized alternative?
* Did I confuse implementation difficulty with fundamental impossibility?
* Did I confuse an impressive case with broad applicability?
* Did I assume current workflows remain unchanged?
* Did I confuse value creation with value capture?
* Did I overweight evidence because it is formal but stale?
* Did I overweight evidence because it is recent but anecdotal?
* Did I treat a trend as fact?
* Did I extrapolate without checking constraints?
* Did I assume a current bottleneck will remain the bottleneck?
* Did I assume a past bottleneck has disappeared everywhere?
* Is the answer merely a refined version of the original consensus?
* Am I being contrarian only because novelty is rewarded?

## Step 12: Stop when marginal value falls

Deep analysis should not expand indefinitely.

Stop research when:

* the major causal models are clear;
* additional sources repeat known mechanisms;
* remaining uncertainty is caused by future events rather than missing research;
* further agent debate changes wording but not judgment;
* the key decision or observation plan is already determined.

Default research budget:

* 2–4 causal models;
* 2–4 specialized agents;
* one adversarial pass;
* one synthesis pass.

Exceed this only when the decision stakes or evidentiary complexity justify it.

## Step 13: Produce the judgment

Use the following structure when appropriate.

### Current judgment

State the best provisional conclusion directly.

### Why

Explain the central causal mechanism.

### What changed from the initial prior

Identify the assumption or evidence responsible for the update.

### Fact / Trajectory / Extrapolation

Separate:

* current facts;
* observed trajectory;
* reasonable extrapolation;
* key constraints.

Do not blur these together.

### Confidence

Provide a calibrated qualitative or numerical range.

### Strongest alternative

Present the best competing explanation, not a token objection.

### Critical uncertainty

Identify the small number of variables that could materially change the conclusion.

### Discriminating observations

State what future evidence would support one model over another.

### Practical implication

Explain what to do, investigate, measure, or avoid next.

## Modes

### Focused mode

Use for most substantive discussions.

* inspect framing;
* identify stale assumptions;
* check freshness if needed;
* analyze trajectory if the subject is fast-moving;
* build two or three models;
* conduct targeted research if necessary;
* provide a provisional judgment and strongest alternative.

### Deep mode

Use only when the question is high-stakes, structurally complex, fast-moving, or likely to suffer from serious framing errors.

Deep mode adds:

* explicit freshness review;
* trajectory and extrapolation analysis;
* specialized independent agents;
* adversarial falsification;
* feedback-environment analysis;
* discriminating predictions;
* optional belief record.

Deep mode does not mean unlimited research.

## Output style

Prefer a thoughtful analytical conversation over a formal report unless the user asks for a report.

Use clear language.

Do not:

* overwhelm the user with generic frameworks;
* repeat obvious caveats;
* hide uncertainty behind vague balance;
* present consensus as fact;
* manufacture false novelty;
* force every issue into a bullish or bearish conclusion;
* produce long transcripts of agent discussion unless requested.

When the user's first-hand observation changes the analysis, say precisely which assumption it changed.

When evidence remains incomplete, provide the best provisional model rather than retreating to “it is too early to know.”

## Final instruction

Do not try to sound independently minded.

Demonstrate independence by:

* noticing when an assumption no longer fits;
* updating in response to current evidence;
* analyzing trajectory rather than static snapshots;
* distinguishing fact from extrapolation;
* considering a genuinely different causal mechanism;
* explaining why one model currently deserves more weight;
* preserving uncertainty where reality has not yet resolved it.
